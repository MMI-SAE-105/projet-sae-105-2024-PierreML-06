Black Phone

a psychological thriller between

reality and the supernatural

Black Phone, directed by Scott Derrickson and released

in 2021, is a film that skillfully blends horror and

psychological elements. Adapted from a short story

by Joe Hill, Stephen King's son, the film stands out

for its tense atmosphere and its exploration of deep

fears related to childhood.

watch the trailer

A captivating plot

The story follows Finney Shaw (Mason Thames),

a young boy kidnapped by a serial killer,

the "Grabber" (Ethan Hawke). Imprisoned in a

soundproof basement, Finney discovers a disconnected

phone that starts to ring.

He realizes that the voices of the Grabber's previous victims

are speaking to him through the phone, offering him advice

on how to escape his captivity.

This original premise combines the supernatural with a

desperate survival situation,

making the film intriguing.

A blend of genres

Black Phone stands out for its ability to juggle

between different genres. The film is primarily a

thriller, but it also incorporates elements of

fantasy and horror.

Derrickson manages to build palpable tension

that rises as Finney learns to navigate this

desperate situation.

The direction is careful, with an artistic direction

that enhances the oppressive atmosphere,

while the immersive soundtrack plunges the

audience into the protagonist's anxiety.

An exploration of

trauma

Beyond the horror, Black Phone explores deep

themes like trauma, resilience, and the fear of the unknown. The film highlights

the relationship between Finney and his sister Gwen

(Madelyn McGraw), who uses her dreams to find him.

This sibling connection emphasizes the importance

of love and solidarity in the face of adversity,

adding an emotional dimension that humanizes

the story and makes Finney's quest even more

desperate.

A reflection on

technology and the future

Black Phone illustrates how technology,

even old technology, shapes our fears and connects

the past to the future. The disconnected phone symbolizes

a connection between the living and the dead, between

the tragic past of the victims and Finney’s hope.

This film evokes the "prehistoric future", where ordinary

objects take on new meaning in our relationship with technology and the invisible.

Once a symbol of communication, the phone here becomes a dystopian tool, both a

salvation and a curse. Black Phone reminds us that

technology can play a central role in our modern

narratives, reactivating buried fears and

projecting uncertainties about our future.
